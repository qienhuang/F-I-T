>### Why F-I-T

I attempt to answer the same question in a unified way:
From quantum and molecules to cells, individuals, organizations, nations, and civilizations—why do clearly defined hierarchical structures emerge? Why does evolution often manifest as a repeating rhythm of "oscillation—stability—aggregation—re-stability"? Why do many systems fail not because of insufficient power or lack of information, but because the "pace of doing things" is wrong?

I ultimately compressed "evolution" into three minimal variables:

*   **Force (F)**: The action that drives or constrains system change (interactions, selection pressures, institutional constraints, objective function gradients).
*   **Information (I)**: Structures that can persist in time and produce causal effects (codes, forms, patterns, models).
*   **Time (T)**: Not a background scale, but a spectrum of characteristic time scales (rhythms) emergent from the interaction of F and I.

**F-I-T is a meta-framework, not a theory of a specific domain.**
Its purpose is: to first reduce any problem of "evolution, development, origin, collapse, innovation" to `(F, I, τ)`, and then discuss levels, critical points, and transition paths.

>### **F-I-T (Force-Information-Time) System Evolution Meta-Framework v1.0**

#### **I. Core Definitions and Basic Propositions**
1.  **What is F-I-T**: It is a meta-framework for observing, analyzing, and explaining the **evolution of any complex system**. It posits that system evolution can be deconstructed into the interaction of three fundamental elements: **"Force"**, **"Information"**, and **"Time"**.
2.  **Basic Proposition**: **Specific 'Forces' (F) act upon a system, shaping or selecting specific 'Information' structures (I); once formed, this structure possesses relative stability and persists within its characteristic 'Time' scale (T), while simultaneously reacting back upon the force field.** Evolution is the process of continuous interaction and iteration among these three.

#### **II. Connotations of the Three Core Elements**
1.  **Force (F)**:
    *   Broadly refers to **any energy, pressure, or rule that drives system change or constrains the direction of its change**. It can be physical (gravity, energy gradients), biological (natural selection), cognitive (curiosity), social (competition, institutions), or algorithmic (recommendation rules).
    *   Key Attributes: **Directionality and Intensity**.

2.  **Information (I)**:
    *   Broadly refers to **any structure, pattern, or code within a system that can reduce uncertainty and possesses a certain degree of persistence**. It is not just data, but **organized forms with function**. Examples include DNA sequences, biological organs, legal systems, linguistic symbols, algorithmic models, market conventions.
    *   Key Attributes: **Stability, Replicability/Transmissibility, Functionality**.

3.  **Time (T)**:
    *   Not an absolute background, but an **intrinsic property endogenous to the system's evolutionary process**. Systems at different levels have **characteristic time scales** matched to their "refresh rates" and "resonance frequencies" (e.g., molecular vibrations use femtoseconds, civilization evolution uses millennia).
    *   Key Attributes: **Scalability and Relativity**. The "flow of time" perceived at different levels is fundamentally different.

#### **III. Five Basic Principles of the Framework**
1.  **Hierarchical Nesting Principle**: The complex world is composed of nested levels (e.g., physical, chemical, biological, cognitive, social, civilizational). Each level is an **emergent product** of the **information structures** of the level below it, while simultaneously serving as the **foundational platform** for the action of **force and information** of the level above it.
2.  **Cross-Level Transition Principle**: When bottom-up interactions driven by specific "forces" reach a critical point, their "information structure" undergoes a **phase transition**, emerging with entirely new, irreducible properties, thereby birthing a new level with its own F-I-T coordinates (e.g., from molecules to cells, from language to nations).
3.  **Multi-Level Time Coupling Principle**: Different levels operate at their inherent characteristic time scales (refresh rates). Evolution is driven by the **coupling of processes across multiple time scales**: fast variables (high-frequency refresh) provide power and variation, while slow variables (low-frequency refresh) provide stable structure and selection pressure. Macro-evolution is the result of a symphony of multiple temporal rhythms.
4.  **Cyclical Reinforcement Principle**: "Force" shapes "Information", and once "Information" solidifies, it becomes a new "Force" (constraint or driver), influencing the next round of evolution. Forming an evolutionary cycle of **"F → I → (new)F → (new)I..."**. Civilization is the product of the accelerated accumulation of this cycle.
5.  **Path Dependence Principle**: The trajectory and endpoint (attractor) of system evolution strongly depend on its initial conditions (initial information structure) and the specific historical perturbations (accidental forces) experienced at bifurcation points. History is irreversible.

#### **IV. Analysis Flow Using F-I-T**
1.  **Define System and Level**: Clarify the object you want to analyze (e.g., a meme, a technology, a civilization) and identify the level it resides in and the relevant levels above and below.
2.  **Decompose Elements**:
    *   **Force (F)**: What are the current primary driving and constraining forces? (e.g., platform algorithms, market competition, physical limits)
    *   **Information (I)**: What is the core structure or code of the system? (e.g., meme templates, technical standards, legal clauses)
    *   **Time (T)**: What is the time scale of key processes? (e.g., trending cycles, product iteration cycles, dynastic cycles)
3.  **Establish Dynamic Correlations**: Describe how F affects the rate and direction of change in I; how I adapts to or stores the influence of F; how T regulates the rhythm of this process.
4.  **Observe Cross-Level Interactions**: Consider how the lower level provides a foundation for the upper level (e.g., individual behaviors converging into social trends), and how the upper level sets rules for the lower level (e.g., laws constraining individuals).
5.  **Identify Critical Points and Transitions**: Look for imbalances in "forces" or instability points in "information" structures within the system, and analyze the possibility of the emergence of new structures.

#### **V. Major Mathematical and Thinking Tools**
1.  **Dynamical Systems Theory** (Phase Space, Attractors, Bifurcation Theory): Modeling evolutionary trajectories in state space.
2.  **Coupled Oscillator Models**: Studying how subsystems with different frequencies (refresh rates) synchronize or resonate.
3.  **Agent-Based Modeling (ABM)**: Simulating macro-level emergence starting from bottom-up interactions.
4.  **Network Science**: Describing the propagation of information and force within complex structures.
5.  **Multi-Scale Analysis and Singular Perturbation Theory**: Handling systems with separated fast and slow variables.

#### **VI. Significance, Limitations, and Your Deeper Questions**
*   **Significance**: F-I-T provides a **unified conceptual grammar** that connects evolutionary narratives across physics, biology, society, and technology, enabling us to use the same logic to understand everything from cell division to the rise of AI.
*   **Limitations**: It is currently primarily a **qualitative meta-framework and mental model**. To generate quantitative predictions, it must be combined with theories and data from specific domains.

## Abstract
We present the Force–Information–Time (FIT) Framework, a minimal vocabulary intended to describe and test evolutionary dynamics in adaptive systems (physical, biological, cognitive, and social) at an explicitly chosen level of description. FIT is built on five primitives—**State**, **Time**, **Force**, **Constraint**, and **Information**—plus operational estimators that make the primitives measurable in toy systems and simulations. From these primitives we formulate six framework principles, distinguishing (i) definitional relationships (true by construction) from (ii) empirically testable hypotheses about constraint accumulation and attractor formation in dissipative or optimizing regimes. FIT yields **18 falsifiable propositions** organized around (A) low-force-variance attractors, (B) information–constraint tradeoffs, and (C) phase-transition signatures. We provide a **solvable toy model** showing how monotone accumulation of an effective constraint variable can imply collapse of force variance, and we give reproducible operational metrics for two computational testbeds (Conway’s Game of Life; Langton’s Ant). The primary contribution is not a claim of universal correctness, but a compact, testable language for comparing evolutionary phenomena and for generating concrete refutation tests.

**Keywords**: evolutionary dynamics; constraints; information theory; attractors; phase transitions; complexity; optimization dynamics

## 1. Introduction

### 1.1 Motivation: the “fragmentation” problem
Evolution-like dynamics appear in many domains: thermodynamic relaxation, chemical self-assembly, Darwinian adaptation, learning in neural networks, and institutional change. Each field has successful local theories, but cross-domain comparison is hindered by incompatible primitives and measurement choices (e.g., “fitness”, “loss”, “free energy”, “selection pressure”, “incentives”). FIT is a proposal to standardize *how we talk* about such dynamics before we argue about mechanisms.

### 1.2 Design goal
FIT aims to be:

1. **Minimal**: few primitives, reusable across domains.
2. **Operational**: each primitive has at least one measurement estimator in concrete testbeds.
3. **Falsifiable**: generates propositions that can be refuted in well-defined systems.
4. **Level-aware**: always explicit about the chosen state representation and coarse-graining.

### 1.3 What this paper contributes (concretely)
- Five primitives with explicit notation, plus suggested estimators.
- Six framework principles, partitioned into definitional vs. empirical hypotheses.
- A dependency chain that makes assumptions explicit.
- A solvable toy model that demonstrates the internal consistency of “constraint accumulation ⇒ force-variance collapse” under simple dynamics.
- 18 falsifiable propositions with three expanded exemplars and test protocols.
- Two computational testbeds (GoL; Langton’s Ant) mapped into FIT variables with measurable proxies.

### 1.4 What this paper does *not* claim
- It does **not** claim FIT is a final or universal theory.
- It does **not** claim the primitives are the only possible primitives.
- It does **not** present comprehensive empirical validation across real-world domains.

The paper is designed so that critics can produce counterexamples quickly.

## 2. Scope, notation, and “level of description”

### 2.1 System and state
A **system** is represented at a chosen level of description by a state variable:
- Discrete: $S_t \in \mathcal{S}$
- Continuous: $S_t \in \mathbb{R}^d$

The choice of $S_t$ is part of the model: FIT is **level-aware**. A claim about $S_t$ is not automatically a claim about microphysics.

### 2.2 Time
Time is an ordered index set $t \in \mathcal{T}$. FIT supports:
- Discrete time: $t \in \{0,1,2,\dots\}$
- Continuous time: $t \in \mathbb{R}_{\ge 0}$

### 2.3 Force (generalized drift)
A **force** is any directed influence that biases state change at the chosen level. Operationally, it is a drift term (deterministic or expected) in the system’s evolution:
- Discrete: $\mathbb{E}[S_{t+1} - S_t \mid S_t] = \alpha\,F(S_t,t)$
- Continuous: $\frac{dS}{dt} = \alpha\,F(S,t) + \text{noise}$

This generalizes physical forces, fitness gradients, loss gradients, incentives, and prediction errors—*provided they act as directional drivers in the chosen state space*.

### 2.4 Constraints (effective restrictions)
A **constraint** is a restriction that reduces accessible transitions or reduces effective degrees of freedom at the chosen level.

Two equivalent ways to represent constraints:

1. **Transition constraint** (graph view): a set of forbidden transitions $E_{\text{forbidden}}$ on a state transition graph.
2. **Effective constraint measure** (statistical view): a scalar $C_t$ estimating how "collapsed" or "restricted" the trajectory distribution is in state space.

This paper uses **effective constraints** because they are measurable in toy systems without requiring omniscient knowledge of forbidden transitions.

### 2.5 Information (measured distinctions)
Information is any scalar functional that quantifies distinguishability, uncertainty, or predictive structure of the state. Depending on domain, examples include:
- Shannon entropy $H(S_t)$
- Mutual information $I(S_{t}; S_{t+\Delta})$ ("predictive information")
- Kolmogorov complexity proxies via compression
- Fisher information in parametric families

FIT does **not** force one information definition; instead it requires authors to declare which estimator is used.

## 3. The five primitives with minimal working examples (MWE)

Each primitive below includes (i) a definition at FIT level, and (ii) an MWE showing how one could measure it.

### 3.1 Primitive P0: State $S$
**Definition**: The complete configuration of the system at time $t$ at the chosen level of description.

**MWE (Langton's Ant)**:  
$S_t = (x_t, y_t, \theta_t, G_t)$, where $G_t$ is the grid coloring configuration.

### 3.2 Primitive P1: Time $T$
**Definition**: An ordering that indexes state changes.

**MWE (Conway's Game of Life)**:  
$t$ is the generation index; the update map is deterministic: $S_{t+1} = f(S_t)$.

### 3.3 Primitive P2: Force $F$
**Definition**: A directional driver of expected state change in the chosen state space.

**MWE (gradient descent)**:  
- $S_t = w_t$ (parameters)  
- $F(S_t) = -\nabla_w L(w_t)$  
- $w_{t+1} = w_t + \eta F(w_t)$

**MWE (chemotaxis)**:  
- $S_t = x_t$ (position)  
- $F(x_t) \propto \nabla \text{[glucose]}(x_t)$

### 3.4 Primitive P3: Constraint $C$
**Definition (effective constraint)**: A scalar estimator of how restricted the system's accessible or realized state variability is at time $t$.

**Operational estimators (choose one):**
- **Frozen fraction**: fraction of components that do not change over a time window.
- **Intrinsic dimension**: estimated manifold dimension of recent trajectory points.
- **Compression ratio**: compressed_size / raw_size for a windowed state trace.
- **Rank/covariance collapse**: $-\log \det(\Sigma_t)$ for covariance $\Sigma_t$ of state samples.

**MWE (finite-state machine)**:  
If the number of reachable states drops from $N$ to $N_{\text{reachable}}(t)$ due to forbidden transitions, define

$$
C_t := \log N - \log N_{\text{reachable}}(t).
$$

(Units: bits, if log base 2.)

### 3.5 Primitive P4: Information $I$
**Definition**: A scalar quantifying distinguishability/uncertainty/predictive structure of the state.

**Operational estimators (choose one):**
- **State entropy**: $I_t := H(S_t)$ (requires a distribution estimate).
- **Predictive information**: $I_t := I(S_{t-k:t}; S_{t:t+k})$ .
- **Compression-based**: $I_t := -\text{compressedSize}(S_{t-k:t})$ up to constants.

**MWE (DNA base identity)**:  
$S\in\{A,T,G,C\}$, uniform prior ⇒ $H(S)=2$ bits.

## 4. FIT principles and dependency chain

We separate **definitional principles** (true by construction once estimators are chosen) from **empirical hypotheses** (testable claims about dynamics in common regimes).

### 4.1 Principle 1: Directed evolution
**Statement**: In the chosen state representation, expected change has a directional component aligned with net force:

$$
\mathbb{E}[\Delta S_t \mid S_t] = \alpha F(S_t,t) + \epsilon_t,
$$

where $\epsilon_t$ captures noise/unmodeled effects.

**Interpretation**: “Force” is the generalized drift.

### 4.2 Principle 2: Constraint bounds effective state variability
**Statement**: If $C_t$ is an effective constraint estimator defined from trajectory variability, then (by definition) increasing $C_t$ corresponds to reduced realized variability in $S_t$ at the chosen level.

**Interpretation**: This is a measurement convention: you must define $C_t$ so it tracks restriction/collapse.

### 4.3 Principle 3: Coarse-grained time asymmetry
**Statement**: In dissipative, coarse-grained descriptions, the map from past to future is typically many-to-one, so inversion is not unique:

$$
S_{t+1}=f(S_t) \text{ does not imply a unique } S_t=f^{-1}(S_{t+1}).
$$

**Interpretation**: FIT does not claim microscopic irreversibility; it claims coarse-grained irreversibility is typical.

### 4.4 Hypothesis 4: Effective constraint accumulation in stationary regimes
**Statement**: In many stationary-environment adaptive or optimizing regimes, effective constraints increase over time until an attractor/plateau:

$$
C_{t+\Delta} \gtrsim C_t \quad \text{for large enough } t.
$$

**Notes**:
- This is **empirical**, not a logical truth.
- It depends on the chosen estimator and boundary of the system.
- Counterexamples (cyclic dynamics, perpetual exploration, non-stationary environments) are expected and are valuable for scoping FIT.

### 4.5 Hypothesis 5: Low-force-variance attractors
**Statement**: In such regimes, as effective constraints approach a plateau, force variance collapses:

$$
\mathrm{Var}[F(S_t,t)] \to 0 \quad \text{as } C_t \to C_{\infty}.
$$

We call such plateaus **low-force-variance attractors**. “Nirvana state” is shorthand for this technical condition; it is descriptive, not normative.

### 4.6 Hypothesis 6: Information–constraint scaling near attractors
**Statement**: Near a low-force-variance attractor, predictive information per constraint saturates:

$$
\frac{I_t}{C_t} \to k \quad \text{as } t\to\infty \text{ (in stationary regimes)},
$$

for some system-dependent constant $k$, under a declared information estimator.

### 4.7 Dependency chain
At the level of assumptions:

```
(State S, Time T) 
   + (Force as drift)  ──>  Principle 1
   + (chosen estimators) ──> Principle 2
   + (coarse-graining)  ──>  Principle 3
   + (stationary + dissipative/optimizing regime) ──> Hypothesis 4
   + (Hypothesis 4 + stability) ──> Hypothesis 5
   + (Hypothesis 4–5 + chosen info estimator) ──> Hypothesis 6
```

## 5. A solvable toy model (worked result)

This section provides a minimal demonstration that “constraint accumulation ⇒ force-variance collapse” can be internally consistent and analytically solvable.

### 5.1 Model definition
Let the state be one-dimensional $x(t)\in\mathbb{R}$ with a stable fixed point at $x^*$. Define the force as:

$$
F(t) := x(t)-x^*.
$$

Assume linear relaxation (a standard, stable dissipative dynamics):

$$
\frac{dx}{dt} = -\kappa\,F(t) = -\kappa(x-x^*), \quad \kappa>0.
$$

Define an **effective constraint accumulator** as the time-integral of squared force:

$$
C(t) := C_0 + \gamma\int_0^t F(\tau)^2 d\tau, \quad \gamma>0.
$$

This $C(t)$ is monotone and captures the idea "the system's path accumulates stabilizing structure / lock-in proportional to how much force was present during relaxation." (Operationally, many constraint estimators increase as variability collapses; this is a stylized proxy.)

### 5.2 Closed-form solution
Solve the relaxation:

$$
F(t)=F_0 e^{-\kappa t}, \quad F_0:=x(0)-x^*.
$$

Then

$$
C(t)=C_0 + \gamma\int_0^t F_0^2 e^{-2\kappa\tau}\,d\tau
= C_0 + \frac{\gamma F_0^2}{2\kappa}\left(1-e^{-2\kappa t}\right).
$$

Define

$$
C_\infty := \lim_{t\to\infty} C(t) = C_0 + \frac{\gamma F_0^2}{2\kappa}.
$$

Eliminate time $t$ to relate force magnitude to constraint accumulation:

$$
F(t)^2 = F_0^2 e^{-2\kappa t} = \frac{2\kappa}{\gamma}\left(C_\infty - C(t)\right).
$$

### 5.3 Interpretation
- $C(t)$ is monotone and approaches a finite plateau $C_\infty$.
- Force variance (in this deterministic model, $F^2$) collapses **linearly** with distance to the constraint plateau.
- This toy model realizes Hypotheses 4–5 under transparent assumptions.

### 5.4 How to falsify the analogy
If you claim a domain follows this mechanism, you must identify:
- what corresponds to $x$, $F$, and $C$,
- and whether your measured $C_t$ actually behaves monotonically and predicts collapse of $\mathrm{Var}(F)$.

## 6. Eighteen falsifiable propositions

**Important**: Every proposition below requires you to declare:
1) the state representation $S_t$,  
2) a force estimator $F(\cdot)$,  
3) a constraint estimator $C_t$, and  
4) an information estimator $I_t$.

We group the propositions into three clusters.

### Cluster A: Low-force-variance attractors (P1–P6)
- **P1 (Attractor persistence / "nirvana irreversibility")**: Once a trajectory enters a low-force-variance attractor (below threshold $\epsilon$) it does not exit without an exogenous perturbation that measurably alters constraints or forces.
- **P2 (Late-time constraint non-decrease)**: After burn-in, $C_t$ is non-decreasing up to small fluctuations in stationary regimes.
- **P3 (Force-variance decay form)**: In stationary regimes, $\mathrm{Var}[F]$ decreases as an exponential or power law; the best-fit family is consistent within a system class.
- **P4 (Attractor detection criterion)**: Low-force-variance attractors can be detected by the joint condition: small $\mathrm{Var}[F]$ and small finite differences $\Delta C_t,\Delta I_t$ over a window.
- **P5 (Perturbation recovery)**: Return time to the attractor after small perturbations decreases as $C_t$ increases.
- **P6 (Multi-attractor landscapes)**: Systems with non-convex force fields exhibit multiple low-force-variance attractors; basin measures can be estimated and are stable in stationary regimes.

### Cluster B: Information–constraint tradeoffs (P7–P12)
- **P7 (Entropy capacity bound, discrete)**: If constraints restrict accessible states to a set $\mathcal{S}_t^{\text{acc}}$, then $H(S_t)\le \log |\mathcal{S}_t^{\text{acc}}|$. (For continuous systems replace cardinality by volume + units.)
- **P8 (Predictive information saturation)**: In stationary regimes near an attractor, predictive information $I_{\text{pred}}(t)$ saturates while $C_t$ approaches a plateau.
- **P9 (Compressibility increases near attractors)**: Windowed state traces become more compressible as $C_t$ increases.
- **P10 (Constraint estimator equivalence class)**: Different reasonable constraint estimators (frozen fraction, intrinsic dimension, compression ratio, covariance collapse) are monotonically correlated in the same regime.
- **P11 (Regime change signature)**: Phase transitions or qualitative regime shifts produce a discontinuity/peak in derivatives of ratios like $I_t/C_t$, or a peak in force autocorrelation time.
- **P12 (Information growth requires constraint reconfiguration)**: Sustained increases in predictive information require either (i) new constraints, or (ii) relaxation/reorganization of old constraints; “information growth without structural change” is bounded.

### Cluster C: Phase transitions and scaling (P13–P18)
- **P13 (Critical slowing down)**: Near a transition point $C=C_c$, relaxation time scales as $\tau \propto |C-C_c|^{-\nu}$ for some $\nu>0$.
- **P14 (Scale-free fluctuations near criticality)**: Force or state fluctuations show approximate power-law spectra near $C_c$.
- **P15 (Universality within primitive/estimator class)**: Systems sharing comparable primitives and comparable estimators show similar scaling exponents near criticality.
- **P16 (Constraint hierarchy)**: Constraints decompose into layers with distinct timescales; slow constraints modulate fast forces.
- **P17 (Dimensional collapse)**: Estimated intrinsic dimension of the trajectory manifold decreases as $C_t$ increases toward a plateau.
- **P18 (Time-scale separation near attractors)**: Near the attractor, force dynamics equilibrate faster than constraint dynamics: $\tau_F \ll \tau_C$.

## 7. Three exemplar propositions with concrete test protocols

### 7.1 Exemplar 1: P1 (Attractor persistence) in Conway’s Game of Life

**Goal**: validate that once a still-life is reached, force variance remains below threshold in the absence of external perturbations.

**State**: binary grid $S_t \in \{0,1\}^{N\times N}$.  
**Time**: generation index $t$.  
**Force estimator** (cell-level mismatch): define a local force magnitude as deviation from stability conditions (one practical choice):
- For dead cell: $f = |3 - n|$
- For live cell: $f = |n - 2.5|$ (2.5 centers the survival band at 2–3)

Then define global force variance as $\mathrm{Var}[f]$ over cells (or over updates).  
**Constraint estimator**: frozen fraction over a window $W$: fraction of cells with zero variance across $W$ steps.  
**Information estimator**: entropy of $2\times 2$ blocks (coarse-grained symbols).

**Protocol**:
1. Initialize random grid. Run for $T_{\max}$ steps.
2. Detect candidate still-life by checking $S_{t+1}=S_t$ for $W$ consecutive steps.
3. Record $\mathrm{Var}[f]$ and $C_t$ before and after detection.
4. Continue for $T_{\text{test}}$ additional steps with no perturbation.
5. **Falsification**: if the configuration leaves the still-life (changes) without external perturbation, or $\mathrm{Var}[f]$ exceeds threshold persistently, P1 fails for this estimator choice.

**Why this matters**: GoL is fully specified and cheap to simulate, so it is a high-leverage falsification target.

### 7.2 Exemplar 2: P11 (Regime change signature) in Langton’s Ant

**Known qualitative fact**: Langton’s Ant exhibits a long chaotic transient before entering a “highway” regime with regular motion.

**State**: $S_t = (x_t,y_t,\theta_t,G_t)$.  
**Force estimator**: directional alignment of step vectors over a window (alignment increases in highway regime).  
**Constraint estimator**: trajectory predictability over a window (e.g., $R^2$ of linear fit, or entropy rate estimate).  
**Information estimator**: entropy (or compression) of local grid patterns over a window.

**Protocol**:
1. Run the ant for $T$ steps.
2. Compute the time series of $I_t/C_t$ (or its derivative) and force alignment statistics.
3. Detect change points by peaks in $|\Delta(I/C)|$ or by change-point detection on alignment.
4. **Prediction**: the detected change point aligns (within tolerance) with onset of highway behavior under standard initial conditions.
5. **Falsification**: if no robust signature appears across reasonable estimator choices, P11 is weakened.

### 7.3 Exemplar 3: P13 (Critical slowing down) in a generic transition model

**Minimal observable**: relaxation time $\tau$ from small perturbations.

**Protocol (model-agnostic)**:
1. Identify an order parameter $m(t)$ for the regime of interest (domain specific).
2. Apply small perturbations (noise impulses) at multiple times.
3. Measure recovery time $\tau(t)$ as the time for $m(t)$ to return within a band.
4. Estimate $C_t$ (your chosen constraint measure).
5. Fit $\tau \propto |C-C_c|^{-\nu}$.
6. **Falsification**: absence of diverging $\tau$ near any candidate $C_c$ indicates P13 does not hold for this system/estimator class.

## 8. Validation program and success criteria

### 8.1 Tier-1 (ground-truth computational systems)
- Conway’s Game of Life
- Langton’s Ant
- Ising model (optional, for criticality tests)

**Success criterion**: a subset of propositions (P1, P4, P11, P13, P17) should be testable and should produce stable measurement behavior across estimator variants.

### 8.2 Tier-2 (mechanistic simulated systems)
- Boids/flocking
- Simple evolutionary algorithms
- Reinforcement learning in toy environments

**Success criterion**: estimator correlations (P10) and regime shift signatures (P11) should appear robustly.

### 8.3 Tier-3 (noisy real-world domains)
- Biological time series
- Neural network training curves
- Social/economic indicators

**Success criterion**: if FIT adds value, it should provide early-warning diagnostics (P13/P11) that are at least competitive with baseline domain methods.

---

## 9. Relation to prior frameworks (positioning)

FIT is intended to be complementary to established theories by providing a *common outer vocabulary* rather than replacing mechanisms.

- **Free Energy Principle / Active Inference**: can be expressed as a specific choice of state, force (free-energy gradients), and information estimator.
- **Constructor Theory**: emphasizes possible/impossible transformations; FIT emphasizes measured trajectory dynamics and estimators.
- **Physical/algorithmic complexity measures (e.g., mutual information, compression)**: can instantiate FIT’s information and constraint estimators.

This paper does not attempt to “derive” those frameworks; it supplies a bridge language to compare them empirically.

---

## 10. Applications (sketches, not claims)

- **AI safety**: define “termination” or “stability” criteria using P4 (attractor detection) and monitor constraint reconfiguration under distribution shift.
- **Institutional design**: treat policies as constraints and study when incentives (forces) produce stable vs. brittle equilibria.
- **Complexity science**: use P11/P13 as generic regime-shift diagnostics when mechanistic models are unavailable.

Each application is a hypothesis generator and requires domain-specific formalization.

---

## 11. Limitations and open questions

1. **Estimator dependence**: FIT statements are only as meaningful as the declared estimators for $F, C, I$.
2. **Boundary dependence**: “constraint accumulation” can invert depending on system boundary and coarse-graining.
3. **Non-stationarity**: In changing environments, Hypotheses 4–6 may not hold.
4. **Microscopic reversibility**: FIT does not resolve debates about the arrow of time at the fundamental level.
5. **Quantum/relativistic extension**: out of scope here; requires different state and information formalisms.

---

## 12. Conclusion
FIT proposes a minimal, operational, falsifiable vocabulary—State, Time, Force, Constraint, Information—to describe evolutionary dynamics in adaptive systems at an explicit level of description. The framework’s value is measurable by how easily it can be *used* and *refuted*. We provide (i) an analytically solvable toy model, (ii) 18 concrete propositions, and (iii) two computational testbeds with operational metrics to initiate community testing.

## Why tempo matters

Many complex systems fail not because they lack power or information, but because **high‑impact changes become irreversible faster than the system can correct them**.

FIT treats tempo (correction timescales) as a first‑class variable.
